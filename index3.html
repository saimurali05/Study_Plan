Ideal daily schedule
6:00 – 7:00 AM
Plan
Plan the day
Read today's topic from the roadmap. Write exactly what you will build — no ambiguity. Open Notion or a notebook. No phone.
7:00 – 9:00 AM
Learn
Deep learning block
Watch / read the topic. Phone in another room. Take notes in your own words — never copy-paste. Understand the why, not just the how.
9:00 – 11:00 AM
Build
Build block 1 — close all tutorials
Open VS Code. Build from memory. Break things. Fix them yourself before searching. This discomfort is exactly where learning happens.
11:00 – 12:00
Rest
Break + food
Walk outside. Eat properly. No screen if possible. Your brain consolidates learning during rest — this is not optional.
12:00 – 2:00 PM
Build
Build block 2 — polish and deploy
Add error handling. Write a README. Push to GitHub. Deploy to Render or Vercel. Something must be live on the internet by end of this block.
2:00 – 3:00 PM
Review
Debug and code review
Read your code out loud. Ask: what would break in production? What edge case did I miss? Fix the top 2 issues.
3:00 – 4:00 PM
Learn
Free study block
Docs, YouTube deep dives, Stack Overflow rabbit holes. Follow genuine curiosity. This is where you find the 10% that isn't in any tutorial.
8:00 – 8:30 PM
Post
Post publicly + reflect
Write your LinkedIn post: "Day X — built Y. Here's what I learned." 3 sentences is enough. Note one mistake to fix tomorrow. Then close the laptop.

The non-negotiable rules
01
4 hours minimum — every single day
2 hours deep learning, 2 hours building. Split them any way but hit both. Weekends included for the first 90 days. No exceptions.
02
Something must exist that didn't exist before
Code file, deployed app, GitHub commit, LinkedIn post, client DM sent. If nothing was created, the day doesn't count in your head or this tracker.
03
Post publicly every evening — zero exceptions
"Day 12 — built a streaming chatbot. Here's what I learned." Three sentences is enough. Consistency beats quality every time. The audience you build here becomes your first customers.
04
Close the tutorial before building
Watching tutorials feels like learning. It's not. Real learning starts the moment you close the tab and try to rebuild what you watched from memory. That discomfort is the gap closing.
05
No social media during learning blocks
Phone in another room. Instagram and Twitter after 8pm only. One context switch costs you 23 minutes of deep focus. Guard every minute of your learning blocks like money.
06
One skill, one roadmap, 365 days — no pivoting
You will see ads for "learn X in 30 days" and "this new skill is better." Ignore all of it. The person who finishes one roadmap completely will always beat the person who starts five halfway.
07
Deploy everything, no matter how ugly
A working ugly app beats a perfect app that only lives on localhost. The moment something is live on the internet, it can get users, feedback, and money. Perfectionism is procrastination.
